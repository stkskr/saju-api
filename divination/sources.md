# Sources and provenance

This library draws on public-domain classical material plus original synthesis. Below is what was used and where the original texts can be verified.

## I Ching

- **James Legge, *The Yî King***, 1882 (Oxford / Sacred Books of the East). Fully public domain. Used as the structural skeleton for hexagram judgments and images. The actual prose in this library is rewritten into modern English — Legge's Victorian phrasing was not reproduced.
- Original Chinese text 周易: [Chinese Text Project](https://ctext.org/book-of-changes) / [Wikisource](https://zh.wikisource.org/wiki/周易).
- Richard Wilhelm's German translation (later rendered to English by Cary F. Baynes, 1950) is *not* public domain and was not used.

### A note on copyright

English translations of these classics by modern authors (Joseph Yu, Joey Yap, Sherwood Tu, and others) are under copyright and were not consulted. The techniques themselves — combinations, clashes, useful god selection, pattern theory, twelve life stages, nayin, sin sal lookups — are ancient knowledge passed down for centuries and are not subject to anyone's copyright. They are described here in fresh modern English.

### Korean Saju tradition

Korean Saju (사주) is methodologically identical to Chinese Bazi but layered with Korean-specific terminology, romanization, and some interpretive emphases (particularly around 신살 and family-pillar relationships). The same source texts apply; Korean schools cite them under romanized names (예: 자평진전).

## How to verify

For any specific claim in this library, cross-check against the Chinese original at ctext.org or Wikisource. If you find a factual error, treat the original classical text as authoritative.
